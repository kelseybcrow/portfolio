About:

Coding first captured my interest when I moved to Seattle to study creative writing.
I imagined myself writing a literary masterpiece holed up inside indie coffee shops in one endless stretch of rainy afternoons.
But then I kept hearing about this cutting-edge new skill called coding. It seemed almost mystical, like real honest-to-goodness magic. I was itching to learn more about it, but I worried it would never be attainable for someone like me.
I didn't fit the image of those nerdy comp-sci guys I saw at school. I never took advanced math classes in high school, and I was… duh, duh, duh… an “English major” – an entity scorned by so many STEM students.
But then one evening after class, I ventured to a Women in Tech event at a swanky downtown tech startup. As I listened to the presentations and met tons of successful female programmers, a new dream was born.
Here were real-life female programmers right in front of me. They looked like me and they talked like me. They were passionate about coding and they were good at it. My brain exploded.
I started going to Women Who Code educational events and made friends with other women who also wanted to learn this crazy new magic. I was on fire, and I considered joining one of the many coding bootcamps in Seattle.
Then life got in the way. To make a long story short, I succumbed to the nasty societal programming that women don’t belong in STEM. This cultural mentality is so insidious and far-reaching, it took me years to crawl out from under it.
But now I’m calling the *BS* and I’m going all out to pursue my dream of becoming a programmer. Coding is way too crazy-cool to give up on. I’m finally in a coding bootcamp and I’m loving it, and there are even other women in the bootcamp with me!
I wish I'd been encouraged to pursue a STEM career back when I was a young girl, but I'm so thankful that I finally found my way here as an adult.
